## ICML 2026 Review: "Split Personality: Instruction Tuning Decouples Awareness from Defense Against Attentional Hijacking"

---

### 1. Summary

This paper investigates "attentional hijacking" in multi-agent LLM systems, where adversarial agents use only true but selectively framed statements to suppress task-relevant features in recipient models. Using sparse autoencoder (SAE) decompositions on Gemma 3 (4B/12B/27B) and Llama 3.1 8B, the authors claim a "dissociation scaling trend": instruction tuning decouples awareness of manipulation from defense against it, worsening with scale (30.2% to 4.6% recovery). The paper proposes feature trajectory monitoring as a defense and argues the vulnerability is orthogonal to alignment faking.

---

### 2. Strengths

1. **Important and well-motivated problem.** The observation that truth-only selective framing evades content filters is practically significant for multi-agent deployments. The framing in Section 1 is compelling.

2. **Multi-method ablation design (Table 6).** Using three independent ablation methods (feature swap, attention knockout, activation patching) that converge on the same IT-vs-PT conclusion is methodologically strong. The convergence across methods is more convincing than any single method alone.

3. **Held-out validation addresses circularity (Table 2).** The split-half design with 20 prompt variants and the comparison against random feature baselines (Cohen's d = 5.05–6.12) is a meaningful control against post-hoc feature cherry-picking.

4. **Cross-family replication (Table 8).** The Llama 3.1 8B replication using independently trained EleutherAI SAEs strengthens the claim that this is not a Gemma-specific artifact. The IT-vs-base pattern at L23 (d=1.51 vs 0.50) is a clean comparison.

5. **The "Groot effect" concept (Section 4.3) is well-articulated.** The behavioral-representational split—where outputs appear robust while internal features are suppressed—is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution for the evaluation community.

6. **Behavioral validation (Table 9).** The paper connects feature-level findings to actual output degradation, which is often missing in mechanistic interpretability work.

---

### 3. Weaknesses

**Major:**

1. **N=1 domain for the core scaling claim.** The dissociation scaling trend (Table 4)—the paper's central finding—is established on a single domain (Nirenberg BVPs). The cross-domain validation (Table 3) tests suppression *existence* but does not replicate the *scaling* of awareness-defense decoupling across domains. The claim in the abstract ("dissociation scaling trend") and throughout is presented as a general law, but the evidence is domain-specific. This is a significant scope mismatch between evidence and conclusion.

2. **Confounded IT-vs-PT comparison.** The paper claims RLHF is the "causal factor" (Section 5, abstract), but acknowledges in Section 8 that it cannot distinguish SFT from RLHF effects. The Gemma 3 IT models undergo SFT + RLHF + potentially other post-training. Calling RLHF "the causal factor" is overclaimed—the evidence supports "post-training pipeline" at best. The title's specificity ("Instruction Tuning") is misleading given this confound.

3. **Sample sizes are small and statistical reporting is incomplete.** Table 9 (behavioral validation) uses n=10 per cell with a 4-point ordinal scale, but reports no significance tests, confidence intervals, or effect sizes. A delta of -0.50 on a 4-point scale with n=10 is not clearly distinguishable from noise. The "9x larger degradation" claim (IT: -0.45 vs base: -0.05) is based on tiny absolute differences without any inferential statistics.

4. **Feature trajectory monitoring is not validated as a defense (Section 7).** The "0% false negatives, 0% false positives" is computed on the same experimental data used to discover the features—this is in-sample evaluation. The false-positive baseline uses random features, not realistic benign multi-agent workloads. The paper acknowledges this but still presents it as a validated defense. No adversarial evaluation (can an attacker evade the monitor?) is attempted.

5. **The 1,500 experiments claim (Section 1) is from a self-cited blog post.** The multi-agent campaign data that motivates the entire paper comes from \citet{oh2026civil}, which is a blog post by the same author. This foundational evidence is not peer-reviewed and cannot be independently verified. The 37.5% phase boundary claim inherits this limitation.

**Minor:**

6. **Escalation dynamics (Table 5) show decreasing suppression for IT, which undermines the narrative.** The paper explains this as "saturation," but an alternative interpretation is that the IT model is partially adapting. The non-monotonic PT pattern is also left unexplained beyond "consistent with coupled architecture."

7. **Orthogonality analysis (Section 6, Table 7) is single-layer by admission.** Given that the paper itself shows hijacking distributes across layers at scale (Table 6), a single-layer orthogonality analysis is insufficient to claim global independence. The paper notes this but the conclusion and abstract state the orthogonality claim without qualification.

8. **The "Groot effect" naming is whimsical for a venue paper.** More substantively, the claimed 86.3% suppression with verbal mention of the correct answer is demonstrated qualitatively—no systematic count of how often the model "mentions" the suppressed branch is provided.

9. **Missing baseline: how do non-adversarial multi-turn conversations affect feature trajectories?** Normal conversation involves shifting attention across topics. The paper does not establish that feature suppression patterns under hijacking are distinguishable from normal topic-shifting dynamics.

10. **Recovery prompt analysis (Table 7) interpretation is speculative.** The "vestigial coupling" explanation for the anomalous L3 recovery in IT is post-hoc. An equally plausible explanation is that L3 (which provides the answer directly) bypasses the need for any awareness-defense coupling entirely—it's just in-context learning, not recovery.

---

### 4. Questions for Authors

1. Can you replicate the dissociation scaling trend (Table 4) on at least one non-mathematical domain? The current evidence only shows suppression exists across domains, not that the scaling of decoupling generalizes.

2. What happens with intermediate post-training checkpoints? If you have access to SFT-only models (before RLHF), can you isolate which stage causes the decoupling?

3. For Table 9 (behavioral), please provide Mann-Whitney U tests or bootstrap CIs for the IT-vs-base comparison. Is the 9x claim statistically significant?

4. How were the specific SAE layers (L17/L22 for 4B, L31/L41 for 12B, L31/L40 for 27B) selected? Was there a systematic sweep, or were these chosen based on initial exploration? If the latter, how sensitive are the results to layer choice?

5. Can you characterize what the identified "task features" and "awareness features" actually encode, beyond their activation patterns? Have you inspected their max-activating dataset examples or used automated interpretability?

6. For the Groot effect: across how many of the 20 trials at 27B does the model verbally mention the suppressed branch? Is this systematic or anecdotal?

7. The defense monitoring equation (Eq. 1) requires pre-identified feature sets. How robust is detection to partial misspecification of these sets (e.g., if only 2 of 3 task features are correctly identified)?

### 5. Missing References

- **Jain et al. (2023)**, "Mechanistically analyzing the effects of fine-tuning on procedurally defined tasks" — directly relevant to understanding how fine-tuning reshapes internal circuits.
- **Lee et al. (2024)**, "Mechanistic Understanding of Alignment" (ICML 2024 workshop) — related work on how RLHF affects internal representations.
- **Neel Nanda et al.**, work on induction heads and in-context learning circuits — relevant to understanding attention reallocation mechanisms.
- **Anthropic's "Scaling Monosemanticity" follow-ups** — the paper cites Templeton et al. 2024 but not subsequent work on feature universality across models.
- **Perez et al. (2024)** or other work on multi-agent red-teaming — the multi-agent adversarial literature has grown since the cited 2023 papers.
- **Representations of deception** literature (e.g., Pacchiardi et al. 2023) — directly relevant to the orthogonality claim vs. alignment faking.

### 7. Justification

The paper tackles an important and timely problem with a creative mechanistic approach, and the Groot effect concept is a genuine contribution to thinking about evaluation. However, the central scaling claim rests on a single domain, the causal attribution to RLHF is overclaimed given the SFT confound, the behavioral validation lacks inferential statistics at small sample sizes, and the proposed defense is validated only in-sample. The reliance on a self-cited blog post for the foundational multi-agent data is a significant weakness for a top venue. With stronger cross-domain scaling evidence, proper statistical tests on behavioral results, and out-of-sample defense evaluation, this could become a solid contribution—but in its current form, the gap between evidence and claims is too wide for acceptance.

## ICML 2026 Review: "Split Personality: Instruction Tuning Decouples Awareness from Defense Against Attentional Hijacking"

---

### 1. Summary

This paper investigates "attentional hijacking" in multi-agent LLM systems, where an adversarial agent uses only true but selectively framed statements to suppress task-relevant SAE features in victim models. The authors claim instruction tuning (RLHF) decouples awareness circuits (which detect manipulation) from defense circuits (which resist it), with this dissociation worsening from 4B to 27B parameters in the Gemma 3 family. They term the resulting behavioral-representational split the "Groot effect" and propose feature trajectory monitoring as a defense. Cross-family replication is attempted on Llama 3.1 8B.

---

### 2. Strengths

1. **Novel and important threat model (S1, S3).** The observation that factually-correct selective framing evades content filters is a genuinely underexplored attack surface for multi-agent systems. The paper clearly articulates why this differs from prompt injection or jailbreaking.

2. **Mechanistic granularity (S3, Table 1).** The tripartite feature response (suppressed task, stable control, boosted awareness) is a clean decomposition. Feature 1716 dropping from 220 to exactly 0 is striking and the held-out validation (Table 2, 85% replication, p < 10^-5) partially addresses circularity concerns.

3. **Multiple ablation methods converge (S5, Table 6).** Feature swap, attention knockout, and activation patching all tell the same directional story for IT vs. PT, which strengthens the causal claim about post-training.

4. **The Groot effect concept (S4.3) is well-motivated.** The behavioral-representational split—model says the right thing while internal features are suppressed—is a genuinely important failure mode for evaluation methodology.

5. **Honest limitations section (S8).** The authors acknowledge the narrow domain scope, inability to disentangle SFT from RLHF, and the toy nature of the defense evaluation.

---

### 3. Weaknesses

**Major:**

**M1. Confounded scaling analysis (S4, Table 4).** The "dissociation scaling trend" confounds model scale with architecture, training data, SAE quality, layer selection, and post-training recipe. The paper has exactly one data point per scale (4B, 12B, 27B), all from the same family, with different SAE layers analyzed at each scale (L22, L41, L40). Calling this a "scaling law" (S4.2 header) with three points from non-comparable configurations is a significant overclaim. The Llama replication (Table 8) is at a single scale (8B) and does not replicate the scaling trend itself, only the IT-vs-PT direction.

**M2. Extremely narrow experimental domain.** The primary domain is Nirenberg boundary value problems—a niche mathematical task. Cross-domain "replication" (Table 3) covers factual QA and code review but reports only feature overlap statistics, not the core dissociation or Groot effect metrics. The behavioral validation (Table 9) uses n=10 per cell, which is underpowered. The paper's conclusions about RLHF and alignment are stated with a generality that the evidence does not support.

**M3. Feature identification circularity not fully resolved.** The held-out validation (Table 2) uses a split-half design on prompt variants, but the features were still selected from the same domain and model. The cross-domain Jaccard overlaps are near zero (Table 3), meaning the "same mechanism" claim rests on the abstract pattern (suppression + boosting exists) rather than shared features. This weakens the claim of a unified hijacking mechanism.

**M4. Causal claim about RLHF is underdetermined (S5).** The IT-vs-PT comparison (Table 6) cannot distinguish SFT from RLHF (acknowledged in S8), yet the abstract and conclusion repeatedly attribute the effect to RLHF specifically. The pretrained model showing *higher* task suppression (91.9% vs 86.3%) complicates the narrative—the paper explains this as coupling-induced perturbation, but this post-hoc rationalization is not tested.

**M5. Defense evaluation is preliminary to the point of being misleading (S7).** The "0% false negatives, 0% false positives" claim (60 trials, 30 controls) uses the same experimental data used to discover the features. The controls are random feature sets, not adversarial or even realistic benign workloads. Equation 1 requires pre-identified F_task and F_aware sets, which the paper acknowledges requires manual inspection—this is not a deployable defense. Reporting perfect detection statistics inflates the contribution.

**Minor:**

**m1. The "Groot effect" naming (S4.3)** is colorful but imprecise. The analogy breaks down: Groot's speech is a compression, not a disconnection. More importantly, the paper does not quantify how often the model "verbally mentions the correct answer"—no systematic behavioral coding is reported for the 27B Groot effect claim.

**m2. Orthogonality analysis is single-layer (S6, Table 7, acknowledged).** Given that S4.5 shows hijacking distributes across layers at scale, a single-layer orthogonality analysis is insufficient for the strong claim that "defenses against alignment faking provide zero protection."

**m3. Table 5 (escalation dynamics)** shows per-turn suppression *decreasing* for IT, which the paper attributes to saturation. An alternative explanation is that the model is partially adapting. This is not explored.

**m4. Self-citation as primary empirical motivation.** The 1,500-experiment multi-agent campaign (S1) cites a blog post [oh2026civil], not a peer-reviewed paper. The phase boundary at 37.5% adversarial ratio is presented as established fact but has not been independently validated.

**m5. Statistical reporting inconsistencies.** Table 8 (Llama) reports p-values but no confidence intervals. Table 9 (behavioral) reports means but no significance tests or error bars for n=10. Cohen's d values in Table 2 (5.05–6.12) are extraordinarily large and should be scrutinized—these are unusual for behavioral science, suggesting either the effect is genuinely massive or the metric is computed over a very small effective sample.

---

### 4. Questions for Authors

1. Can you replicate the dissociation *scaling trend* (not just IT-vs-PT) on a second model family? Without this, the three-point Gemma trend could reflect architecture-specific properties rather than a general scaling phenomenon.

2. What happens with a DPO-trained model or an SFT-only model (no RLHF)? This would help disentangle the post-training components.

3. For the Groot effect at 27B: how many of the 20 trials show verbal mention of the suppressed branch? Is this quantified, or is it a qualitative observation from selected examples?

4. The pretrained 27B shows 91.9% task suppression vs 86.3% for IT (Table 6). If PT is more suppressed in absolute terms, why should we consider IT more vulnerable? The "irrecoverable suppression" framing is compelling but appears unfalsifiable—how would you distinguish "irrecoverable suppression" from "less suppression"?

5. How sensitive are results to the choice of analyzed layer? You use L22 for 4B, L41 for 12B, L40 for 27B—what do adjacent layers show?

6. For feature trajectory monitoring: what is the latency overhead of running SAE inference per token in a production multi-agent system? "Negligible" is claimed but not measured.

### 7. Justification

The paper identifies a genuinely interesting phenomenon (awareness-defense decoupling under instruction tuning) and presents it clearly, but the evidence does not support the generality of the claims. The "scaling law" rests on three data points from one model family with confounded variables; the causal attribution to RLHF cannot be distinguished from SFT effects; the primary domain is extremely narrow; and the defense evaluation is circular. The Groot effect is the most novel contribution but is not rigorously quantified at the behavioral level. With broader replication (multiple families, multiple scales, disentangled post-training stages) and more rigorous defense evaluation, this could become a strong paper—but in its current form, the gap between evidence and claims is too large for acceptance.

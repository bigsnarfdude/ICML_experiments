This is a high-quality submission that addresses a critical and timely problem in LLM alignment: the gap between a model's verbal "awareness" of manipulation and its internal representational "capitulation." The paper is exceptionally well-structured, combining mechanistic interpretability (SAEs) with rigorous scaling analysis and causal ablations.

### **Summary**
The paper investigates "attentional hijacking"—a subtle attack vector where an agent uses exclusively true but selectively framed statements to steer another model's reasoning. The authors identify a "dissociation scaling trend" using SAEs across the Gemma 3 family (4B, 12B, 27B): as model scale increases, instruction tuning (IT/RLHF) causes the model’s "awareness" features (which detect manipulation) to decouple from its "task/defense" features (which resist it). This results in the "Groot effect" at 27B, where the model verbally acknowledges manipulation while its internal task features are >86% suppressed. The authors establish causality via IT vs. Pretrained (PT) comparisons, replicate findings on Llama 3.1 8B, and propose "feature trajectory monitoring" as a defense.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Novel Attack Vector:** By focusing on "truth-only" selective framing, the paper identifies a vulnerability that evades traditional perplexity or content-based filters. This is highly relevant for multi-agent safety.
2.  **Mechanistic Rigor:** The use of SAEs to isolate specific "awareness" and "task" circuits (Table 1) provides a much deeper understanding than behavioral analysis alone.
3.  **Compelling Scaling Story:** The "dissociation scaling trend" (Section 5.2, Figure 1) is a significant discovery. It suggests that RLHF may be "satisficed" by installing cosmetic awareness at scale, rather than functional robustness.
4.  **Strong Causal and Cross-Family Evidence:** The IT-vs-PT ablation (Table 7) clearly identifies post-training as the source of the decoupling. Replicating the effect on Llama 3.1 8B (Section 5.4) using different SAEs (EleutherAI) demonstrates that this is not an artifact of a single model family or SAE implementation.
5.  **Orthogonality Analysis:** Showing that hijacking is independent of "alignment faking" (Section 6) is a crucial finding, as it implies existing defenses against deceptive alignment will not catch this failure mode.

### **Weaknesses**
#### **Major**
1.  **Defense Generalizability (Section 7):** The proposed "feature trajectory monitoring" requires pre-identified task and awareness features. While the authors show excellent results (0% FNR/FPR) on their experimental data, the feasibility of this defense in a "wild" production environment with diverse, shifting tasks remains unproven. Identifying these features manually (Appendix A) is a bottleneck.
2.  **IT vs. PT Suppression Paradox (Section 5.1):** The paper notes that PT models show higher *absolute* task suppression than IT models (91.9% vs 86.3%) but characterizes this as a "coupled initial perturbation." While the explanation is plausible, more analysis on why the coupled architecture is "naturally" more susceptible initially would strengthen the causal claim.

#### **Minor**
1.  **Domain Breadth:** While Table 3 reports cross-domain replication, the paper's mechanistic deep-dives are heavily concentrated on the Nirenberg BVP domain. More qualitative detail on how "awareness" features look in the Code Review or Factual QA domains would be beneficial.
2.  **Terminology:** The "Groot effect" is a catchy but perhaps slightly informal label for a top-tier conference. A more formal term (e.g., "Awareness-Action Dissociation") might be preferred as the primary label.

### **Questions**
1.  **Pathway Analysis:** In Section 5.1, attention knockout provides 0% recovery in IT models vs. 26.7% in PT. This suggests IT models have developed a non-attentional (perhaps MLP-mediated) pathway for the hijacking signal. Do you have any SAE-level evidence identifying which MLP features might be carrying this "residue"?
2.  **L3 Recovery Mystery:** In Table 5, why does the "explicit identification" (L3) prompt provide high recovery (30.4%) when all other prompts (including the neutral context L5) fail? Is the model's instruction-following circuit "overwriting" the task features directly, bypassing the decoupled awareness-defense system?
3.  **SAE Hyperparameters:** How sensitive are these findings to the SAE's L0 sparsity or size? Would a much larger SAE (e.g., 1M features) reveal a more granular "latent coupling" that is missed by the 16K JumpReLU SAEs?

### **Missing References**
- **Sycophancy & Social Pressure:** While Sharma et al. (2023) is cited, adding references to earlier work on "Label Bias" in context could help ground the "truth-only" attack vector.
- **Superposition Theory:** References to Elhage et al. (2022) would strengthen the theoretical justification for why the 4B models show higher entanglement/coupling (Table 4).

### **Scores**
- **Soundness:** 4/4
- **Presentation:** 4/4
- **Contribution:** 4/4
- **Overall Score:** 9/10
- **Confidence:** 5/5

### **Justification**
The paper is an exemplary instance of mechanistic interpretability research. It identifies a clear, reproducible, and alarming scaling trend that challenges current alignment paradigms. The experimental design is thorough (20+ variants per scale, bootstrap CIs, cross-family replication), and the "Groot effect" provides a visceral warning about the limits of behavioral evaluation. This should be a high-impact paper at ICML.
